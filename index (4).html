01 How Prop Firms Really Make Money ○ Not started ▶

Before learning to trade, understand the business you're walking into. A prop firm (FTMO, FundedNext, The5ers, E8, and hundreds of others) sells you a challenge: pay a fee of roughly €50–€600, hit a profit target (usually 8–10%) on a demo account without breaking their loss rules, and they "fund" you with a larger account where you keep 70–90% of profits.

Follow the money

Most prop firms earn the majority of their revenue from challenge fees, not trading profits. The economics only work because most people fail. Industry data and firms' own disclosures suggest that only around 5–10% of buyers ever pass, and far fewer ever receive a payout. Many funded accounts are also still simulated — the firm pays you from fee revenue and copies only its best traders into real markets.

Reality check

This is not a scam by default — reputable firms do pay out real money to consistent traders. But the product is designed so that impatient, under-prepared traders fund the winners. Your entire job is to not be the impatient one.

The rules that fail people

  • Profit target: typically 8–10% in Phase 1, 4–5% in Phase 2.
  • Maximum daily loss: usually 5%. Breach it once — even for a second, even on a floating loss — and you fail instantly.
  • Maximum overall drawdown: usually 10% (sometimes "trailing", which is much harsher — it follows your equity peak upward).
  • Consistency rules: some firms fail you if one day produces too large a share of your profit.
  • News restrictions: many firms void trades opened within minutes of major news releases.

Notice something: every rule punishes aggression. The challenge is not a test of prediction. It is a test of whether you can make steady gains without ever having one bad day. That is why this course is mostly about risk, not about "signals".

Correct: fees from the ~90% who fail are the core business model. Knowing this keeps you from re-buying challenges emotionally.

02 Market Basics: What You're Actually Trading ○ Not started ▶

Most prop-firm traders trade forex (currency pairs like EUR/USD), indices (like US30 or NAS100), or gold (XAU/USD) through CFDs. You never own anything — you're speculating on price moving up ("long") or down ("short").

Leverage is the killer

Leverage doesn't create profit — it multiplies both directions. Retail CFD brokers in the EU must disclose loser rates: typically 70–80% of retail accounts lose money, and oversized leveraged positions are the main reason. Your position size, not your prediction, decides whether you survive.

Sessions and when to trade

Markets move in sessions. For someone in Ireland this is convenient: the London session (8:00–16:30) and the London/New York overlap (13:30–16:30 Irish time) have the most movement in EUR/USD, GBP/USD, gold and US indices. Trading two focused hours in the overlap beats staring at charts all day.

Q1 — With 1:100 leverage, a €1,000 account controlling €100,000 of EUR/USD moves against you by 1%. What happened to your account?

Nothing until you close the trade
A 1% market move on 100× exposure equals 100% of your capital. This is why position sizing (Module 3) matters more than any strategy.
03 Risk Management — The Only Real "Secret" ○ Not started ▶

Here is the entire "secret" professionals actually use, in one sentence: risk so little per trade that no losing streak can hurt you, and let a modest statistical edge compound. Everything else is decoration.

The 1% rule

Never risk more than 1% of the account on a single trade (0.5% during a challenge is even safer). On a $10,000 challenge account, that's $100 maximum loss per trade. With a 5% daily loss limit, you can be wrong five times in one day and still be alive. Risk 2.5% per trade and two bad trades end everything.

Drawdown is asymmetric. Protecting capital isn't cautious — it's mathematically optimal.

04 Reading Charts Without the Mysticism ○ Not started ▶

Charts don't predict the future. They show where buyers and sellers have committed money, which lets you place stops and targets in sensible places. That's all technical analysis needs to do for you.

Candlesticks in 60 seconds

Each candle shows four prices for its time period: open, high, low, close. A candle closing above its open is bullish (buyers won that period); below, bearish. The wicks show where price was rejected. You don't need to memorise 40 pattern names — you need to answer one question per chart: who is in control right now, and where would they be proven wrong?

The only three concepts that matter to start

  • Trend: a market making higher highs and higher lows is trending up. Trade with it, not against it. "The trend is your friend" is a cliché because fighting trends is the fastest way to donate money.
  • Support and resistance: price levels where the market has repeatedly turned. These are where you place entries and stops — just beyond them, not exactly on them (everyone's stops cluster at obvious levels, and price loves to sweep them).
  • Risk-to-reward geometry: if the distance to a sensible stop is 20 pips and the distance to the next resistance is only 15 pips, the trade is bad no matter how pretty the setup looks. Skip it.
On indicators

Moving averages, RSI, MACD — all of them are just averages of past price. They can help you stay objective, but no indicator is an edge by itself. If a YouTube video promises a "95% win-rate indicator", you now know exactly who its business model resembles (see Module 01).

Timeframes for a beginner

Start with the daily and 4-hour charts to read direction, and the 1-hour or 15-minute chart to time entries. Avoid the 1-minute chart entirely — it's noise, and it manufactures overtrading, which is the #1 challenge killer.

Q1 — A setup has a 25-pip stop and 12 pips of room before major resistance. What do you do?
Take it — the pattern looks strong
Take it with double size to compensate
Skip it — the reward doesn't justify the risk
A trade risking 25 to make 12 needs a ~68% win rate just to break even. Geometry vetoes opinion, every time.
05 One Setup, Traded a Hundred Times ○ Not started ▶

Amateurs collect strategies. Professionals trade one boring setup with positive expectancy, over and over, and keep a record proving it works. Here is a complete beginner framework — not because it's magic, but because it's simple enough to execute identically 100 times.

The pullback-in-trend setup

  • 1. Direction (4H chart): price must be making higher highs and higher lows (for longs). If the 4H is sideways or unclear — no trade today. Most days are no-trade days.
  • 2. Location (1H chart): wait for price to pull back to a previous support level or the area of the 20-period moving average. You are buying the dip in an uptrend, not chasing green candles.
  • 3. Trigger (1H/15m): a strong bullish candle closing off that level — evidence buyers defended it.
  • 4. Stop: below the pullback low, plus a small buffer.
  • 5. Target: the previous high, only if it's at least 2× the stop distance away. Otherwise skip.
  • 6. Size: from the Module 03 calculator. Then don't touch the trade. Stop or target — walk away.

Expectancy: the number that decides everything

Your edge in one formula: (Win% × AvgWin) − (Loss% × AvgLoss). A 40% win rate with 1:2 R:R gives (0.4×2R) − (0.6×1R) = +0.2R per trade — a losing-most-of-the-time system that steadily makes money. This is the fact that surprises every beginner: you can be wrong more often than right and still win, if your winners are bigger and your risk is constant.

Backtesting: the unpaid apprenticeship

Before risking anything, scroll back on TradingView and mark 100 historical occurrences of your setup. Record each in a spreadsheet: date, direction, R result. This does two things — it proves (or disproves) the edge, and it shows you what a normal 6-trade losing streak looks like before one happens with money on the line.

The journal rule

Every real trade gets a journal entry: screenshot, reason for entry, result, and one sentence on execution quality. Traders who journal improve; traders who don't repeat the same five mistakes for years. This habit alone is worth more than any paid course.

Q1 — A system wins only 40% of the time with 1:2 reward-to-risk. Over 100 trades it will most likely…
Make about +20R — a solid profit
Lose money — you can't win losing 60% of trades
Break even
40 wins × 2R = 80R gained; 60 losses × 1R = 60R lost; net +20R. Expectancy beats win rate. This is the core arithmetic of professional trading.
06 Psychology: Why Smart People Fail Challenges ○ Not started ▶

Challenges are rarely lost to bad analysis. They're lost in about four predictable emotional states. Learn to recognise them by name — naming the state is half of defusing it.

  • Revenge trading: after a loss, immediately re-entering (often bigger) to "win it back". This is how a controlled −1% day becomes a −5% instant failure. Antidote: a hard rule — after two losses, the platform gets closed for the day. No exceptions, no "one more".
  • Overtrading: boredom trades on days with no setup. Your edge exists only in your setup; every other trade is a donation. Antidote: a written checklist that must be fully ticked before entry.
  • Moving the stop: "it'll come back." Widening a stop converts a planned small loss into an unplanned catastrophe. Antidote: the stop is set at entry and never widened. Ever.
  • Euphoria sizing: after three wins, doubling size because you're "in the zone". Variance doesn't know you're in the zone. Antidote: risk % is fixed in your plan, not adjusted by mood.

Pressure and the unemployed trader

This part matters most

Trading money you need is the single strongest predictor of breaking every rule above. "I must make rent this month from trading" produces oversized positions, revenge trades, and blown accounts — the pressure itself destroys the edge. Trading should be funded only from money whose total loss changes nothing, and lives alongside stable income (a job, teaching, contracting), never instead of it. This isn't moralising — it's performance advice. The traders who pass are the relaxed ones.

A daily routine that protects you

  • Before the session: mark levels, define the one scenario you'd trade, write it down.
  • During: maximum 2 trades. Stop after −2% day or +2% day (banking a green day compounds calm).
  • After: journal, close everything, do something physical. The market reopens tomorrow; it always does.
Q1 — You just took two losses (−2% total) and see a "perfect" third setup. Your plan says stop at two losses. What's the professional move?
Take it — the setup qualifies and you need the recovery
Close the platform — the rule exists precisely for this moment
Take it at half size as a compromise
Rules that bend under emotion aren't rules. The third trade after two losses is statistically your worst-executed trade of the day — that's why the rule exists.
07 Challenge Tactics + The Simulator ○ Not started ▶

Once the fundamentals exist, passing a challenge is a pacing problem. Here's how disciplined traders approach it — and a simulator to test any plan against a thousand random futures before spending a euro.

The pacing playbook

  • Risk 0.5% per trade during challenges. Yes, it's slow. A 10% target at +0.2R expectancy takes ~4–8 weeks of normal trading. Most firms now have no time limit — use that.
  • Never hold a position that could breach the daily limit. Your worst-case open risk should stay under half the daily allowance.
  • Bank the pass, don't style it. At +8% of a +10% target, cut risk to 0.25%. The last 2% is where people blow up trying to finish in one trade.
  • Know the fine print: trailing vs static drawdown, news rules, weekend holding rules, consistency rules, minimum trading days. Failing on a technicality after profitable trading is heartbreak you can read your way out of.
  • Buy one challenge only after 3 consecutive profitable months on demo under the exact same rules. The demo months cost nothing and predict everything.

08 Ireland Practicalities: Tax, Regulation, Scams ○ Not started ▶

Tax (talk to an accountant — but know the shape)

Prop-firm payouts are generally not like personal trading gains: you're typically paid as an independent contractor of the firm, which in Ireland usually means the income is treated as self-employed trading income — registered with Revenue, filed via Form 11, with income tax, USC and PRSI applying. Personal CFD/spread-betting profits are taxed differently again. Rules change and individual situations vary, so before any real payouts arrive, one hour with an Irish accountant is money well spent.

Regulation and choosing firms

  • Prop firms are mostly unregulated — they're selling an evaluation service, not brokerage. Your protection is their reputation, so check payout proof, Trustpilot patterns (look at the 1-star reviews specifically), and how long they've operated. Several well-known firms have collapsed overnight taking pending payouts with them.
  • For any personal live trading, use a broker regulated by the Central Bank of Ireland or another EU regulator (CySEC etc.) — EU rules cap retail leverage (1:30 on major forex) and provide negative balance protection for a reason.
  • The Central Bank of Ireland publishes warnings about unauthorised firms — check the register before sending anyone money.

The scam radar

Trading attracts predators, and unemployed people are their favourite audience. Automatic disqualifiers:

  • Anyone promising fixed returns ("2% per day"), guaranteed passes, or "account management" where you hand over money or logins.
  • Signal groups on Telegram/WhatsApp, "copy my trades" schemes, and mentors whose income is selling mentorship rather than trading.
  • Recovery scams — after a loss, someone contacts you claiming they can recover your funds for a fee. It's the same scammer.
  • Lifestyle marketing: rented Lamborghinis and screenshots are free; audited multi-year track records are almost nonexistent. Behave accordingly.
Q1 — A Telegram mentor with 40k followers offers guaranteed challenge passes for €300. What does this tell you?
It's worth €300 to skip the learning curve
It's a scam — pass rates can't be guaranteed, and firms ban account sharing
It's legitimate if they show payout screenshots
"Guaranteed pass" services either steal the fee or breach firm rules (getting the account terminated). Screenshots are trivially faked. No exceptions to this one.

10 Choosing a Prop Firm: Real Challenge Structures Compared ○ Not started ▶

How "getting in" actually works

There is no application, interview, or CV. You go to the firm's website, pick an account size, pay the fee by card, and receive demo login credentials — usually within minutes. The barrier to entry is zero; the barrier is passing. That's precisely why the industry is full of people who bought in before they were ready. You get in whenever you like; the whole game is arriving prepared.

The two challenge formats

  • Two-step (the standard): Phase 1 targets ~8–10% profit, Phase 2 targets ~4–5%, then you're funded. Cheaper fees, more forgiving pacing, and the format the Module 07 playbook assumes. Start here.
  • One-step / instant funding: a single ~10% target, or immediate "funding" — but with tighter drawdowns (often trailing) and higher fees. Trailing drawdown follows your equity peak upward, so a winning run followed by a normal pullback can fail you while you're in profit. Beginners should avoid trailing-drawdown products entirely.

Why "best firm" is the wrong question

Firms in this industry have collapsed overnight — including large, popular ones — taking traders' pending payouts with them. There is no permanently "best" firm, only firms that are currently paying out. Before buying, spend 30 minutes checking: years in operation, recent payout proof from independent traders, the 1-star reviews on Trustpilot (read what they failed people for), and whether the firm recently changed its rules or payment processors — both are classic distress signals.

The firm-selection checklist

  • Static, not trailing, drawdown — non-negotiable for a beginner.
  • No time limit on the challenge (most major firms removed it — never accept a 30-day deadline; deadlines manufacture overtrading).
  • Clear, written rules on news trading, weekend holding, and consistency — and read them twice. More traders fail on fine print than on markets.
  • Refundable fee — several firms refund your challenge fee with the first payout. Prefer them.
  • Free trial available — FTMO and others offer a free trial challenge. Do the free one first. It's the exact exam environment for €0.
  • Payout terms in writing: frequency (weekly/bi-weekly/monthly), minimum, method, and whether a payout denial process exists. Search "[firm name] denied payout" before paying.

The 20 rules of traders who actually get paid

Preparation

  • 1. No real money until three consecutive profitable demo months under full challenge rules. The market will still be there.
  • 2. One market, one setup, one timeframe combination. Add a second instrument only after your first payout, not before.
  • 3. Backtest 100 samples before demo, journal every trade after. Your spreadsheet is your real mentor.
  • 4. Do the firm's free trial as a dress rehearsal — same platform, same rules, zero cost.
  • 5. Read the full rulebook twice and write your own one-page summary of the failure conditions. Fine print fails more traders than markets do.

During the challenge

  • 6. Risk 0.5% per trade, 0.25% once within 2% of the target. The target has no deadline; your discipline does.
  • 7. Hard daily circuit-breakers: stop at 2 losses, −2%, or +2% — whichever comes first. Green days get banked, not extended.
  • 8. Stop loss set at entry, never widened, never removed. Take-profit set at entry too — then hands off.
  • 9. Keep worst-case open risk under half the daily limit at all times, including overnight and through spreads widening.
  • 10. No trading in the 15 minutes around red-folder news (rate decisions, NFP, CPI) — both because firms void those trades and because spreads eat you alive there.
  • 11. Trade your two best hours (for Ireland: the 13:30–16:30 London/NY overlap), then close the platform. Screen time is not edge.
  • 12. A skipped mediocre setup counts as a win in your journal. Track it.

Mindset & money

  • 13. Fixed risk regardless of mood, streak, or "certainty". The moment size follows feelings, the account follows gravity.
  • 14. After any rule break — even a profitable one — trading stops for 24 hours and the journal gets an incident report. Profitable rule breaks are the most expensive trades you'll ever make; they train the habit that kills you later.
  • 15. Total annual challenge-fee budget decided in advance, from money whose loss changes nothing. When it's spent, the answer is more demo, not more fees.
  • 16. Trading lives alongside income, never instead of it. Needing the money is the one edge-destroyer no strategy survives.
  • 17. Withdraw profits on every payout cycle. Money in a prop account is not yours until it's in your bank.

Career

  • 18. Once funded, change nothing. The funded account is traded exactly like the challenge — same size, same rules. Most funded accounts die in month one from "now I can finally size up".
  • 19. Scale by adding accounts/sizes through the firm's scaling plan, not by raising risk per trade.
  • 20. Review the journal weekly, monthly stats monthly. The trader you are in the spreadsheet is the only trader you are.
